Natural language processing (NLP) is an interdisciplinary subfield of linguistics, computer science, and artificial intelligence concerned with the interactions between computers and human language, in particular how to program computers to process and analyze large amounts of natural language data. The goal is a computer capable of understanding the contents of documents, including the contextual nuances of the language within them.

Tokenization is the process of breaking a stream of text up into words, phrases, symbols, or other meaningful elements called tokens. The list of tokens becomes input for further processing such as parsing or text mining. Tokenization is useful both in linguistics (where it is a form of text segmentation), and in computer science, where it forms part of lexical analysis.

Byte pair encoding (BPE) or digram coding is a simple form of data compression algorithm in which the most common pair of consecutive bytes of data is replaced with a byte that does not occur within that data. The algorithm was first described by Philip Gage in a 1994 article "A New Algorithm for Data Compression" in the C Users Journal. The use of BPE for subword tokenization in natural language processing was introduced in 2015 and has since become the dominant approach for training tokenizers for large language models.

The Viterbi algorithm is a dynamic programming algorithm for obtaining the maximum a posteriori probability estimate of the most likely sequence of hidden states—called the Viterbi path—that results in a sequence of observed events, especially in the context of Markov information sources and hidden Markov models (HMM). The algorithm has found universal application in decoding the convolutional codes used in both CDMA and GSM digital cellular, dial-up modems, satellite, deep-space communications, and 802.11 wireless LANs. It is now also commonly used in speech recognition, speech synthesis, diarization, keyword spotting, computational linguistics, and bioinformatics.

A unigram language model is a type of language model that does not take into account context. More precisely, each token in a sequence is assumed to be drawn independently of all other tokens. Despite this seemingly poor assumption, unigram language models are used extensively in information retrieval and text classification tasks. The unigram language model is often used as a baseline in natural language processing research.

Subword tokenization is a tokenization technique that lies between word-level and character-level tokenization. It splits words into smaller units called subwords or wordpieces. This allows the tokenizer to handle rare words and out-of-vocabulary words by breaking them into more common subword units. Popular subword tokenization algorithms include Byte Pair Encoding (BPE), WordPiece, and SentencePiece.

SentencePiece is an unsupervised text tokenizer and detokenizer mainly for neural network-based text generation systems where the vocabulary size is predetermined prior to the neural model training. SentencePiece implements two segmentation algorithms: byte pair encoding and unigram language model. SentencePiece directly trains a subword model from raw sentences without pre-tokenization.

The attention mechanism is a technique in neural networks that allows the model to focus on different parts of the input when producing each part of the output. It was introduced in the context of machine translation, where the model learns to align source and target words. The attention mechanism has since been applied to a wide range of tasks including question answering, text summarization, and image captioning.

Transformer architecture is a deep learning architecture based entirely on attention mechanisms, dispensing with recurrence and convolutions entirely. It was introduced in the paper "Attention Is All You Need" in 2017. The transformer architecture has become the dominant architecture for natural language processing tasks, and has also been applied to computer vision, audio processing, and other domains.

Large language models (LLMs) are language models consisting of neural networks with many parameters, trained on large quantities of unlabeled text using self-supervised learning. LLMs emerged around 2018 and perform well at a wide variety of tasks. This has shifted the focus of natural language processing research away from training specialized supervised models for specific tasks.

Multilingual tokenization presents unique challenges due to the diversity of writing systems, morphological structures, and character sets across different languages. Latin-based languages benefit from byte pair encoding because their words are composed of a relatively small set of characters. However, languages that use non-Latin scripts such as Chinese, Japanese, Korean, Arabic, Russian, and Hindi may require different tokenization strategies.

The morphological richness of a language significantly impacts tokenization efficiency. Languages like Russian, Turkish, and Finnish have complex morphological systems where a single root word can have dozens of grammatical forms. This means that a tokenizer trained primarily on English data will assign more tokens to equivalent content in these languages, creating what is known as the "token expansion penalty" for non-English text.

Information theory provides the mathematical foundation for understanding tokenization efficiency. Entropy, a measure of information content, can be used to evaluate how well a tokenization scheme captures the statistical structure of a language. A good tokenizer minimizes the expected number of tokens needed to represent a message, which is equivalent to maximizing the average token length in characters.

Dynamic programming is a method for solving complex problems by breaking them down into simpler subproblems. It is applicable when the problem has overlapping subproblems and optimal substructure. The Viterbi algorithm, used in TokeNismo for finding the optimal token sequence, is a classic example of dynamic programming applied to sequence decoding problems.

Rust is a multi-paradigm, general-purpose programming language that emphasizes performance, type safety, and concurrency. It achieves memory safety without a garbage collector using a borrow checker that validates references at compile time. Rust's zero-cost abstractions and direct memory control make it an excellent choice for implementing high-performance tokenizers that must process gigabytes of text per second.
